5 Continuity

Definition 5.1 Open interval
#
\[ x \in (a, b) \iff a {\lt} x {\lt} b \]

\(a = -\infty , b = \infty \) is fine.

Definition 5.2 Open set
#

Given \(X \subseteq \mathbb {R}\). For all \(c \in X\) there exists an open interval in \(X\) containing \(c\).

That is, there exists \(\delta {\gt} 0\), such that

\[ (c - \delta , c + \delta ) \subseteq X. \]
Definition 5.3 Interior point
#

An interior point \(c \in X\) if there exists \((c - \delta , c + \delta ) \subset X\).

Definition 5.4 Closed interval
#
\[ x \in [a, b] \iff a \leq x \leq b \]

(\(a, b \in \mathbb {R}\)).

Lemma 5.5
#

\((x_n) \in [a, b]\) converging with \(\lim _{n \rightarrow \infty }x_n = L\). Then \(L \in [a, b]\).

Proof ▶

Assume \(L \notin [a, b]\); take \(\varepsilon = \min \{ |L - b|, |L - a|\} \). Say \(L {\gt} b\). For all \(x_n\) we have

\begin{align*} |x_n - L| & = |x_n - b + b - L| \\ & = (b - x_n) + (L - b) \\ & {\gt} \varepsilon . \end{align*}

Contradiction to \(\lim _{n \rightarrow \infty }x_n = L\).

Theorem 5.6 Bolzano-Weierstrass
#

\((x_n) \in [a, b]\), with \(a, b \in \mathbb {R}\), has a converging subsequence converging in \([a, b]\).

Proof ▶

\((x_n)\) are bounded which by Theorem 3.8 has a convergent subsequence and by Theorem 5.5.

Definition 5.7 Compact interval
#

Call \([a, b]\) (\(a, b \in \mathbb {R}\)) a compact interval if the interval is bounded and closed.

1 Limits of functions

Definition 5.8 Limit of a function
#

Let \(f : (a, b) \to \mathbb {R}\) be a function. Let \(c \in (a, b)\) and \(f\) is possibly not defined at \(c\). We say

\[ \lim _{x \to c}f(x) = L \]

if for all \(\varepsilon {\gt} 0\) there exists a \(\delta {\gt} 0\) such that

\[ |f(x) - L| {\lt} \varepsilon \]

for all \(x \neq c\) with

\[ |x - c| {\lt} \delta . \]

We also write \(f(x) \to L\) as \(x \to c\).

Definition 5.9 Limit from the right
#
\[ \lim _{x \to c ^{+}}f(x) \text{ same but all } x {\gt} c. \]
Definition 5.10 Limit from the left
#
\[ \lim _{x \to c ^{-}}f(x) \text{ same but all } x {\lt} c. \]
Definition 5.11 Infinite limit
#

\(\lim _{x \rightarrow \infty } f(x) = L\): for all \(\varepsilon {\gt} 0\) there exists a \(k \in \mathbb {R}\) such that

\[ |f(x) - L| {\lt} \varepsilon \qquad \text{for all } x {\gt} k. \]
Proposition 5.12 Limit of a function and sequences
#
\[ \lim _{x \to c}f(x) = L \]
\[ \iff \]

for all sequences \((x_n)\) with \(\lim _{n \rightarrow \infty }x_n = c\) have \(\lim _{n \rightarrow \infty }f(x_n) = L\).

Proof ▶

"\(\implies \)". Assume \(\lim _{x \to c}f(x) = L\). Take \((x_n) \in (a, b)\) \((x_n \neq c)\) with \(\lim _{n \rightarrow \infty }x_n = c\). Take \(\varepsilon {\gt} 0\). Need an \(N\) such that

\[ |f(x_n) - L| {\lt} \varepsilon \]

for all \(n \geq N\). We know there exists a \(\delta {\gt} 0\) such that

\begin{equation} \label{eq:1} |f(x) - L| {\lt} \varepsilon \end{equation}
1

for all \(|x - c| {\lt} \delta \) \((x \neq c)\). Since \(\lim _{n \rightarrow \infty }x_n = c\) there exists an \(N\) such that \(|x_n - c| {\lt} \delta \) for all \(n \geq N\). By 1 \(|f(x_n) - L| {\lt} \varepsilon \) for all \(n \geq N\).

"\(\impliedby \)". By contrapositive. Assume \(\lim _{x \to c}f(x) \neq L\) (or does not exist). Need to find a sequence \(x_n\) where \(\lim _{n \rightarrow \infty }x_n = c\) but \(\lim _{n \rightarrow \infty }f(x_n) \neq L\) (or does not exist). Hence there exists a \(\varepsilon {\gt} 0\) such that for all \(\delta {\gt} 0\) such that there exists an \(x\) with \(|x - c| {\lt} \delta \) but \(|f(x) - L| \geq \varepsilon \). Take the "bad" \(\varepsilon {\gt} 0\). Take \(\delta = 1 / n\), get an \(x = x_n\) with \(|x_n - c| {\lt} \delta = \frac{1}{n}\) but \(|f(x_n) - L| \geq \varepsilon \).

\[ \lim _{n \rightarrow \infty }x_n = c \]

but

\[ \lim _{n \rightarrow \infty }f(x_n) \neq L. \]

This completes the proof by the contrapositive.

Lemma 5.13 Linear combination of limits
#

We have \(\lim _{x \to c}f(x) = L_1\) and \(\lim _{x \to c}g(x) = L_2\). Then

\[ \lim _{x \to c}(af(x) + bg(x)) = aL_1 + bL_2. \]
Proof ▶

Using the previous proposition and applying COLT for sequences.

Lemma 5.14 Product of limits
#

We have \(\lim _{x \to c}f(x) = L_1\) and \(\lim _{x \to c}g(x) = L_2\). Then

\[ \lim _{x \to c}(f(x)g(x)) = L_1L_2. \]
Proof ▶

Take \(x_n \to c\). By COLT for sequences,

\[ \lim _{n \rightarrow \infty }\left[f(x_n)g(x_n)\right] = \lim _{x \to c}f(x_n) \cdot \lim _{n \rightarrow \infty }g(x_n) = L_1L_2. \]
Lemma 5.15 Quotient of limits
#

We have \(\lim _{x \to c}f(x) = L_1\) and \(\lim _{x \to c}g(x) = L_2\) with \(L_2 \neq 0\). Then

\[ \lim _{x \to c}\left(\frac{f(x)}{g(x)}\right) = \frac{L_1}{L_2}. \]
Proof ▶

Using the previous proposition and applying COLT for sequences.

Then

2 Continuous functions

Definition 5.17 Continuity at a point
#

\(f : X \to \mathbb {R}\), \(X = (a, b)\) \(c \in (a, b)\). Call \(f(x)\) continuous at \(x = c\) if

\[ \lim _{x \to c}f(x) = f(c). \]

For all \(\varepsilon {\gt} 0\), there exists \(\delta {\gt} 0\) such that

\[ |f(x) - f(c)| {\lt} \varepsilon \]

for all \(x\) with

\[ |x - c| {\lt} \delta . \]
Proposition 5.18 Continuity and limits
#

\(f(x)\) is continuous at \(x = c\) if and only if

\[ \lim _{n \rightarrow \infty }f(x_n) = f\left(\lim _{n \rightarrow \infty }x_n\right). \]

Proposition 5.19 Continuity of composition
#

Assume \(f\) is continuous at \(c \in X\) and \(g\) is continuous at \(f(c) \in Y\). Then \(g \circ f(x)\) is continuous at \(x = c\).

Proof ▶

Use the sequence criterion: take \(x_n \in X\) with \(\lim _{n \rightarrow \infty }x_n = c\). Need \(\lim _{n \rightarrow \infty }g \circ f(x_n) = g(f(c))\).

Set \(y_n = f(x_n)\) since \(f\) is continuous at \(c\) we have that \(\lim _{n \rightarrow \infty }f(x_n) = \lim _{n \rightarrow \infty }y_n = f(c)\), this sequence, \(f(x_n)\), is in \(Y\). Since \(g\) is continuous at \(f(c)\) we have \(\lim _{n \rightarrow \infty }g(y_n) = \lim _{n \rightarrow \infty }g(f(c)) = \lim _{n \rightarrow \infty }g(f(x_n))\).

3 Great Theorems

Theorem 5.20 Intermediate Value Theorem
#

\(f : [a, b] \to \mathbb {R}\) continuous. with \(f(a) {\lt} f(b)\) (say). Pick \(d \in [f(a), f(b)]\); \(f(a) \leq d \leq f(b)\). Then there exists a \(c \in [a, b]\) (not necessarily unique) such that \(f(c) = d\).

Proof ▶

Pick \(d\), assume \(d {\lt} f(b)\) (otherwise can pick \(c = b\)).

Define the set

\[ X \coloneqq \{ x \in [a, b]; f(x) \leq d\} . \]

\(X \neq \emptyset \), since \(a \in X\) and bounded as a subset of \([a, b]\). Hence has a supremum, \(c\). (By term \(1\)) exists a sequence \(x_n \in X\) such that \(\lim _{n \rightarrow \infty }x_n = c\). \(x_n \in X \subseteq [a, b]\) hence \(c = \lim _{n \rightarrow \infty }x_n \in [a, b]\). By continuity \(\lim _{n \rightarrow \infty }f(x_n) = f\left(\lim _{n \rightarrow \infty }x_n\right) = f(c)\).

Claim: \(f(c) = d\).

Assume not, i.e. \(f(c) {\lt} d\) 2 . Then by problem sheet \(1\) (this term) Q7, there exists a (small) neighbourhood \((c - \delta , c + \delta ) \in (a, b)\) such that \(f(x) {\lt} d\) for all \(x \in (c - \delta , c + \delta )\).

In particular, \(f(c + \delta / 2) {\lt} d\) so \(c + \delta / 2 \in X\) but \(c {\lt} c + \delta / 2\) but \(c = \sup {X}\) contradiction!

So \(f(c) = d\).

Corollary 5.21
#

\(f : I \to \mathbb {R}\) continuous on an interval \(I\). Then the image \(f(I)\) is also an interval.

Proof ▶

An interval \(J\) is a set such that whenever \(x {\lt} y \in J\), then all numbers in between are also in \(J\). Now apply Intermediate Value Theorem.

Use \(x = f(a), y = f(b)\) and apply IVT.

Theorem 5.22
#

\(f : [a, b] \to \mathbb {R}\) is continuous. Then \(f\) takes minimum and maximum on \([a, b]\).

Proof ▶

Only do maximum.

Step \(1\).

\(f\) is bounded above, on \([a, b]\).

Say it did, then given \(n \in \mathbb {N}\), exists \(x_n \in [a, b]\) such that \(f(x_n) {\gt} n\) by Bolzano-Weierstrass there exists a convergent subsequence \(x_{n_i}\) with limit \(c \in [a, b]\), here we use closed interval. So \(f(n_i) \to f(c) \in \mathbb {R}\) with \(f(n_i) {\gt} n_i\), at some point \(n_i {\gt} c\).

Hence \(\sup \{ f(a, b)\} \) exists in \(\mathbb {R}\), \(M = \sup \{ f(a, b)\} \). (By term \(1\)) there exists a sequence \(y_n \in f([a, b])\) such that \(\lim _{n \rightarrow \infty }y_n = M\), but \(y_n = f(x_n)\) by continuity \(\lim _{n \rightarrow \infty }f(x_n) = M\).

\(x_n\) might not converge but by Bolzano-Weierstrass will have a converging subsequence in \([a, b]\) so \(\lim x_{n_i} = c \in [a, b]\).

Then \(f(c) = f(\lim x_{n_i}) = \lim f(x_{n_i}) = \lim y_{n_i} = M\).

Together with the Intermediate Value theorem we get the image of a continuous function on a compact interval is again a compact interval.

Definition 5.23 Continuity on a set
#

Continuity on a set \(X\), for all \(c \in X\) and for all \(\varepsilon {\gt} 0\), there exists \(\delta {\gt} 0\) for all \(x \in X\) with

\[ |x - c| {\lt} \delta \implies |f(x) - f(c)| {\lt} \varepsilon . \]
Definition 5.24 Uniform continuity
#

\(f : X \to \mathbb {R}\) is uniform continuous if for all \(\varepsilon {\gt} 0\) there exists \(\delta {\gt} 0\) such that for all \(x, y \in X\) with

\[ |x - y| {\lt} \delta \implies |f(x) - f(y)| {\lt} \varepsilon . \]

In other words

\[ \forall \varepsilon {\gt} 0, \exists \delta {\gt} 0 \text{ s.t. } \forall x, y \in X, |x - y| {\lt} \delta \implies |f(x) - f(y)| {\lt} \varepsilon . \]
Theorem 5.25
#

\(f : [a, b] \to \mathbb {R}\) continuous on a compact interval. Then \(f\) is uniformly continuous.

Proof ▶

Assume not. There exists \(\varepsilon {\gt} 0\) such that for all \(\delta {\gt} 0\) there exists \(x, y \in X\) with

\[ |x - y| {\lt} \delta \]

but

\[ |f(x) - f(y)| \geq \varepsilon . \]

Take such a "bad" \(\varepsilon {\gt} 0\). So for \(\delta = \delta _n = \frac{1}{n}\), has \(x_n, y_n \in [a, b]\) with \(|x_n - y_n| {\lt} \delta \) but \(|f(x_n) - f(y_n)| \geq \delta \). By Bolzano-Weierstrass for a converging subsequence \((x_{n_i})\) of the \((x_n)\) (since \(x_n \in [a, b]\)) say \(\lim x_{n_i} = x ^{*} \in [a, b]\).

Claim: also \(\lim y_{n_i} = x ^{*}\). Indeed,

\begin{align*} |x ^{*} - y_{n_i}| & = |x ^{*} - x_{n_i} + x_{n_i} - y_{n_i}| \\ & \leq |x ^{*} - x_{n_i}| + |x ^{*} - y_{n_i}| \\ & \to 0 + 0 = 0 \end{align*}

Squeezing gives the claim.

Claim:

\[ \lim f(x_{n_i}) - f(y_{n_i}) = 0. \]

Indeed

\begin{align*} |f(x_{n_i}) - f(y_{n_i})| & = |f(x_{n_i}) - f(x ^{*}) + f(x ^{*}) - f(y_{n_i})| \\ & \leq |f(x_{n_i}) - f(x ^{*})| + |f(x ^{*}) - f(y_{n_i})| \\ & \to 0 + 0 = 0 \end{align*}

by continuity of \(f\) and \(x_{n_i}, y_{n_i} \to x ^{*}\).

So for \(n_i\) sufficiently large

\[ |f(x_{n_i}) - f(y_{n_i})| {\lt} \varepsilon \]

contradiction!
