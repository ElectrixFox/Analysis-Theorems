Given a bounded sequence \((x_n)_{n \in \mathbb {N}}\) define two (related) sequences \((\overline{x}_n)_{n \in \mathbb {N}}\) and \((\underline{x}_n)_{n \in \mathbb {N}}\) as follows let

\begin{align*} \overline{x_n} & = \sup \{ x_m \in \mathbb {R}\, |\, m \geq n\} \\ \underline{x_n} & = \inf \{ x_m \in \mathbb {R}\, |\, m \geq n\} \end{align*}
Lemma 3.9 Limsup and Liminf
#

Let \((x_n)_{n \in \mathbb {N}}\) be a bounded sequence. Then \((\overline{x}_n)_{n \in \mathbb {N}}\) is monotonically decreasing and bounded, and \((\underline{x}_n)_{n \in \mathbb {N}}\) is monotonically increasing and bounded. Furthermore

\[ \lim _{n \to \infty }\underline{x_n} \leq \lim _{n \to \infty }\overline{x_n}. \]
Proof ▶

\((x_n)_{n \in \mathbb {N}}\) is bounded, so there is \(C \in \mathbb {R}\) with \(-C \leq x_n \leq C\) for all \(n \in \mathbb {N}\). Define \(X_n = \{ x_m \in \mathbb {R}\, |\, m \geq n\} \subseteq [-C, C]\) bounded, non-empty set. Infimum and supremum exist, and \(-C \leq \underline{x_n} \leq \overline{x_n} \leq C\). both sequences are bounded. \(X_{n + 1} \subset X_n \implies \overline{x_n}\) is an upper bound for \(X_{n + 1} \implies \overline{x_{n + 1}} \leq \overline{x_n}\) for all \(n \in \mathbb {N}\). Similarly, \(\underline{x_n}\) is a lower bound for \(X_{n + 1} \implies \underline{x_n} \leq \underline{x_{n + 1}}\) for all \(n \in \mathbb {N}\) which implies \(\overline{x_n} - \underline{x_n} \geq 0\). Taking limits gives

\[ \lim \overline{x_n} - \lim \underline{x_n} \geq 0. \]
Definition 3.10 Limsup
#

Let \((x_n)_{n \in \mathbb {N}}\) be a bounded sequence. The limes superior of \((x_n)_{n \in \mathbb {N}}\) is defined as

\[ \limsup _{n \to \infty }{x_n} = \lim _{n \to \infty }\overline{x}_n = \inf _{n \geq 1}\{ \sup {\{ x_m\, |\, m \geq n\} }\} . \]
Definition 3.11 Liminf
#

Let \((x_n)_{n \in \mathbb {N}}\) be a bounded sequence. The limes inferior of \((x_n)_{n \in \mathbb {N}}\) is defined as

\[ \liminf _{n \to \infty }{x_n} = \lim _{n \to \infty }{\underline{x}_n} = \sup _{n \geq 1}{\{ \inf {\{ x_m\, |\, m \geq n\} }\} }. \]
Theorem 3.12
#

Let \((x_n)_{n \in \mathbb {N}}\) be a bounded sequence.

  1. There is a subsequence \((x_{n_j})_{j \in \mathbb {N}}\) with

    \[ \lim _{j \to \infty }x_{n_j} = \limsup _{n \to \infty }{x_n}. \]

  2. There is a subsequence \((x_{n_k})_{k \in \mathbb {N}}\) with

    \[ \lim _{k \to \infty }x_{n_k} = \liminf _{n \to \infty }{x_n}. \]

  3. If \((x_{n_j})_{j \in \mathbb {N}}\) is a convergent subsequence, then

    \[ \liminf _{n \to \infty }{x_n} \leq \lim _{j \to \infty }{x_{n_j}} \leq \limsup _{n \to \infty }{x_n} \]
Proof ▶

Definition 3.13 Cauchy sequence
#

A sequence \((x_n)_{n \in \mathbb {N}}\) is called a Cauchy sequence, if for every \(\varepsilon {\gt} 0\) there exists \(n_0 \in \mathbb {N}\) such that

\[ |x_m - x_n| {\lt} \varepsilon \qquad \forall n, m \geq n_0. \]

Theorem 3.16
#

Let \((x_n)_{n \in \mathbb {N}}\) be a Cauchy sequence. Then \((x_n)_{n \in \mathbb {N}}\) is a convergent sequence.

Proof ▶

By Theorem 3.14 \((x_n)_{n \in \mathbb {N}}\) is a bounded sequence. By Theorem 3.8, there is a convergent subsequence \((x_{n_j})_{j \in \mathbb {N}}\). Let \(x = \lim _{j \to \infty }x_{n_j}\). So given \(\varepsilon {\gt} 0\), there is a \(j_0 \in \mathbb {N}\) with

\[ |x_{n_j} - x| {\lt} \frac{\varepsilon }{2}\qquad \forall j \geq j_0. \]

Since \((x_n)_{n \in \mathbb {N}}\) is a Cauchy sequence, there is an \(n_0 \in \mathbb {N}\) with

\[ |x_n - x_m| {\lt} \frac{\varepsilon }{2}\qquad \forall n, m \geq n_0. \]

Now

\begin{align*} |x_n - x| & = |x_n - x_{n_j} + x_{n_j} - x| \\ & \leq |x_n - x_{n_j}| + |x_{n_j} - x|. \end{align*}

Need \(j \geq j_0\) and \(n_j \geq n_0\) (just choose \(j\) large enough). Then \(|x_n - x| {\lt} \frac{\varepsilon }{2} + \frac{\varepsilon }{2} = \varepsilon \) for all \(n \geq n_0\).
